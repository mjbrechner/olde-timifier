ABOUT
OLDE LETTERS
The Olde-Timifier is a tool to convert modern English text to appear as it was written in an earlier period of the history of the language, switching out contemporary orthographic practices for those of olden times. It does not aim to exactly replicate any one particular moment in history, but rather strives to evoke a quasi-historical feel through the use of the thorn (Þ, þ), which replaces a th, and the long s (ſ), which sometimes replaces a lowercase s. Both letters have a long history of use. Much of this history overlapped, although the conventions of usage, particularly with the intricate practices surrounding the long s, changed over the years. The Olde-Timifier can mimic some of the antiquated orthographic practices using these letters, though otherwise, the grammar, spelling, and overall style will otherwise remain as it was originally entered.
The Olde-Timifier employs two archaic letters from the history of the English language, the thorn and the long s.
THORN (Þ, þ) — This letter appeared in Old English along with the letter eth (Ð, ð) to represent both voiced and voiceless dental fricatives. Both of these sounds are represented in Modern English by the digraph th. While the eth gradually fell out of use in English, the thorn was frequently used during the Middle English period and continued into the Early Modern English era.
With the arrival of movable type printing, the letter y began replacing the thorn, as movable types were imported from areas in which the thorn was not used. This substitution resulted in the word ye frequently replacing þe (i.e. the), which is popularly parodied in modern times in the phrase ye olde, which, of course would have been pronounced in earlier times as the, quite unlike the y sound in the second-person plural pronoun ye.
In the Olde-Timifier, the th digraph can be replaced with a lowercase þ, with an uppercase Þ replacing a capitalized Th or TH.
LONG S (ſ) — The long s is an archaic form of the lowercase s that was used in various ways starting in the medieval period when uppercase and lowercase letters became prevalent. Eventually, various conventions became established that dictated in which situations to choose either ſ and s. The Olde-Timifier uses the following conventions:
  • A short s is applied at the end of each word.
  • Before an apostrophe, a short s is used.
  • In the case of the letter being doubled, the first instance is long and the second short, appearing as ſs.
  • Before and after the letter f, a short s is used, as seen in fs or sf.
  • In other instances, a long ſ is used, except when an uppercase letter is required, in which case S remains the standard.
The rules regarding the use of the long s changed over time, and it eventually fell out of favor with printers in the early 19th century, persisting a little longer in handwriting. It can sometimes be found in modern times, as a character in the International Phonetic Alphabet or as a symbol used in calculus.
